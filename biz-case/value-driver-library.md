# Value Driver Library

A reference library of CX value drivers for use with the `/biz-case` skill. Organized by value type: Revenue, Cost, and Brand & Strategic.

For each driver: the mechanism, typical metrics, indicative impact ranges (based on published research and consulting benchmarks), and key assumptions that must hold for the driver to apply.

---

## REVENUE VALUE DRIVERS

### 1. Conversion Rate Improvement
**Mechanism:** Reducing friction in the purchase, sign-up, or application journey increases the percentage of prospects who complete the desired action.
**Typical metrics:** Conversion rate %, drop-off rate at key funnel steps, cart/application abandonment rate, funnel completion rate
**Indicative impact range:** 5–30% conversion uplift depending on current baseline and severity of friction. Higher end applies when current experience has significant identified friction (e.g., >50% abandonment at a specific step).
**Key assumptions:** Demand exists but is being lost to experience friction (not pricing, product, or awareness issues). The friction point has been specifically identified — broad "improve digital" programs sit at the lower end of the range.
**Evidence to seek:** Current conversion rate at identified friction points, competitor conversion benchmarks, user testing evidence of specific friction.

---

### 2. Customer Retention / Churn Reduction
**Mechanism:** Better experience reduces voluntary churn by addressing the experiential reasons customers leave. Proactive intervention catches at-risk customers earlier.
**Typical metrics:** Churn rate %, customer retention rate, customer lifetime value (CLV), 90-day retention rate for new customers
**Indicative impact range:** 10–25% reduction in experience-driven churn. Note: not all churn is experience-driven — some is price, product, or life stage. Impact applies only to the experience-attributable portion.
**Key assumptions:** Churn research or exit surveys exist to indicate experience as a driver. The intervention targets the specific experience moments that precede churn (early life, complaint, billing event, etc.).
**Evidence to seek:** Exit survey data or churn reason codes, link between NPS/CSAT scores and churn probability, current churn rate and cost of churn.

---

### 3. Upsell / Cross-sell Revenue
**Mechanism:** Trust built through great experience increases customer receptivity to additional products or services. High-trust customers are significantly more likely to respond positively to relevant offers.
**Typical metrics:** Products per customer, upsell conversion rate, revenue per customer, cross-sell revenue as % of total
**Indicative impact range:** 15–40% uplift in upsell/cross-sell conversion when experience trust is high, relative to low-trust equivalent. Bain research indicates customers with top-quartile experience scores have 2.5× the upsell conversion of bottom-quartile.
**Key assumptions:** Product portfolio exists for upsell/cross-sell. Barrier is trust and moment (not product-market fit or pricing). Current upsell efforts are active but underperforming.
**Evidence to seek:** Current products-per-customer rate, upsell conversion rate, correlation between satisfaction scores and upsell acceptance in existing data.

---

### 4. Reduced Price Sensitivity / Premium Positioning
**Mechanism:** Customers with strong experience attachment are less likely to switch on price alone. This enables premium pricing or at minimum protects against price-driven erosion.
**Typical metrics:** Price elasticity, win/loss analysis, willingness-to-pay surveys, price premium vs. competitors
**Indicative impact range:** 5–15% pricing premium sustainable for top-quartile CX performers vs. market average (Forrester and Bain CX benchmarks). Applicable primarily in markets with meaningful premium customer segments.
**Key assumptions:** Market has premium customers who are willing to pay more for better experience. Not purely commodity market. Competitive set is visible and comparable.
**Evidence to seek:** Current price positioning vs. competitors, win/loss data, any pricing power analysis or customer willingness-to-pay research.

---

### 5. Referral Revenue (NPS → Word of Mouth)
**Mechanism:** Higher NPS drives word-of-mouth referrals, reducing customer acquisition cost and bringing in higher-quality customers (referred customers typically have better retention and CLV profiles).
**Typical metrics:** NPS score, referral rate, % of new customers from referral, cost per acquisition (CPA) for referral vs. other channels, referral revenue
**Indicative impact range:** Bain benchmark: every 10-point NPS improvement correlates with 2–7% revenue growth in most industries, primarily through referral and retention. Referral-acquired customers typically have 25% higher CLV and 18% lower acquisition cost.
**Key assumptions:** Promoters have social influence in the addressable market (i.e., they interact with potential customers). Referral mechanism exists (even informal). Current NPS is measurably below industry leaders — improvement headroom exists.
**Evidence to seek:** Current NPS and trend, referral rate (if tracked), CPA by channel, industry NPS benchmarks.

## COST VALUE DRIVERS

### 6. Contact Volume Deflection / Self-Service Adoption
**Mechanism:** Better digital self-service, proactive communication, and first-time-right experiences reduce the volume of inbound contacts that require human handling.
**Typical metrics:** Contact volume per customer per year, digital self-service completion rate, digital containment rate, contacts per issue (are customers calling back for the same issue?)
**Indicative impact range:** 15–40% contact volume reduction for mature self-service programs. 10–20% for targeted proactive communication improvements (e.g., better billing communications reducing billing queries).
**Key assumptions:** Contacts are deflectable — research shows which contacts could be resolved through better digital or proactive communication (not all contacts are — regulatory, relationship, and complex interactions are less deflectable). Self-service capability investment is included in the initiative.
**Evidence to seek:** Contact reason analysis (top call reasons and % of volume), current self-service completion rate, contacts that have a digital equivalent customers aren't using.

---

### 7. First Contact Resolution (FCR) Improvement
**Mechanism:** Resolving customer issues completely in the first interaction reduces repeat contacts, re-handling, escalations, and write-offs.
**Typical metrics:** FCR rate (%), repeat contact rate within 7 days, average handle time (AHT), escalation rate, contacts-per-issue
**Indicative impact range:** A 10-percentage-point improvement in FCR rate typically reduces total contact volume by 8–15% (because repeat contacts represent a significant share of total volume in many contact centres).
**Key assumptions:** Root cause analysis exists or can be done to identify the specific reasons for repeat contacts. Fixes are within the organization's control (process, training, system access, authority — not inherently complex product issues).
**Evidence to seek:** Current FCR rate, repeat contact rate, top reasons for repeat contact, handle time data.

---

### 8. Cost-to-Serve Reduction
**Mechanism:** Streamlining service delivery processes, automating low-value interactions, and removing unnecessary steps reduces the fully-loaded cost of serving a customer.
**Typical metrics:** Cost per transaction, cost per customer per year, FTE per 1,000 customers, unit cost by channel
**Indicative impact range:** 20–35% cost-to-serve reduction for comprehensive end-to-end service redesign programs. 10–15% for targeted process improvement. Automation of specific steps: 40–70% unit cost reduction for the automated step (but only delivers total cost reduction if FTE is redeployed or reduced).
**Key assumptions:** Sufficient volume to justify automation investment. Processes are sufficiently standardizable (high variability limits automation opportunity). Cost baseline is understood — cost-per-transaction data exists or can be modeled.
**Evidence to seek:** Current cost-to-serve baseline, volume data by transaction type, process maps showing unnecessary steps or handoffs, FTE allocation by activity.

---

### 9. Error Rate & Rework Reduction
**Mechanism:** Better-designed processes, clearer customer communication, and system integration reduce errors that require rework, reprocessing, or compensation to customers.
**Typical metrics:** Error rate %, rework cost (FTE time × rate), compensation and goodwill gestures, complaint volumes linked to errors, regulatory breach costs
**Indicative impact range:** 30–60% error reduction from targeted process redesign addressing root causes. Higher end achievable where errors are driven by specific, addressable process failures (e.g., manual re-entry between systems).
**Key assumptions:** Error root causes are primarily process or system driven (not people performance). Root cause analysis has been or can be done. Volume and cost of errors is measurable.
**Evidence to seek:** Error rate and trend data, complaint root cause analysis, cost of rework or compensation, system integration gaps that cause errors.

---

### 10. Employee Productivity
**Mechanism:** Better tools, clearer processes, and reduced burden of managing poor experiences free frontline employee time for higher-value work or reduce the FTE required.
**Typical metrics:** Average handle time (AHT), after-call work time, escalation rate, FTE utilization, tasks per FTE per day
**Indicative impact range:** 10–25% productivity improvement from frontline enablement programs (tools, knowledge, authority). Handle time reductions of 15–30% achievable when systems fragmentation is addressed (agents currently navigating multiple screens per call).
**Key assumptions:** Current productivity is constrained by tools, process, or information access — not hiring, capability, or motivation gaps. FTE savings are realizable (either through headcount reduction, redeployment, or growth absorption without adding headcount).
**Evidence to seek:** Handle time and after-call work time, number of systems agents access per transaction, escalation and transfer rates, agent satisfaction data (agents know where the friction is).

## BRAND & STRATEGIC VALUE DRIVERS

### 11. NPS / Satisfaction Score Improvement
**Mechanism:** Systematic experience improvement in moments that matter drives measurable improvement in relationship satisfaction scores, which are leading indicators of retention, advocacy, and revenue.
**Typical metrics:** Net Promoter Score (NPS), CSAT (Customer Satisfaction Score), CES (Customer Effort Score), transactional survey scores at key moments
**Indicative impact range:** 5–20 NPS point improvement achievable within 18–24 months of a focused experience improvement program, depending on current baseline and breadth of program. Industry benchmarks vary significantly — relative to peers is often more meaningful than absolute movement.
**Key assumptions:** Current NPS is below industry best-in-class (improvement headroom exists). The initiative addresses the specific touchpoints that have the greatest drag on NPS (typically identified from driver analysis). Measurement infrastructure exists to track progress.
**Evidence to seek:** Current NPS and trend, NPS driver analysis (which touchpoints have greatest influence), industry benchmark.

---

### 12. Customer Trust & Brand Equity
**Mechanism:** Consistent, reliable, and emotionally resonant experience builds customer trust — a key driver of long-term brand equity, pricing power, and resilience in crisis.
**Typical metrics:** Brand trust tracking (if exists), brand health surveys, sentiment analysis (NLP on reviews/social), share of voice in positive categories
**Indicative impact range:** Difficult to quantify directly in isolation. Correlated with 5–15% pricing premium and 2–3× higher resilience to negative events for high-trust brands (McKinsey brand trust research).
**Key assumptions:** Brand tracking infrastructure exists or is being established. Trust deficit has been identified as a measurable issue (not just assumed). Initiative addresses the specific experience dimensions driving distrust.
**Evidence to seek:** Brand health tracking data, NPS verbatims mentioning trust, complaint themes related to trust failures, any brand equity research.

---

### 13. Regulatory Risk Reduction
**Mechanism:** Proactive experience improvement in areas of regulatory focus (complaints, accessibility, vulnerable customers, fairness, accuracy) reduces the risk of regulatory action, fines, and remediation costs.
**Typical metrics:** Complaint volumes and Ombudsman referral rates, regulatory breach incidents, regulatory remediation costs, regulatory audit outcomes
**Indicative impact range:** Highly context-specific. Regulatory fines in financial services can range from £100K to billions. Remediation programs (e.g., PPI in UK banking) can exceed hundreds of millions. Even modest regulatory risk reduction has high expected value when downside scenarios are material.
**Key assumptions:** Regulatory risk has been assessed and linked to specific experience failures. The initiative addresses the regulated experience dimensions. Legal/compliance sign-off confirms the risk reduction logic.
**Evidence to seek:** Complaint volumes and trend, Ombudsman referral rate, regulatory correspondence, compliance assessment of current experience.

---

### 14. Employee Engagement & Retention
**Mechanism:** Empowering frontline staff with better tools, clearer processes, and authority to resolve customer issues improves job satisfaction, reduces attrition, and reduces recruitment and training costs.
**Typical metrics:** Employee NPS (eNPS), employee satisfaction score, voluntary attrition rate, recruitment cost per FTE, time-to-productivity for new starters
**Indicative impact range:** 10–20% attrition reduction for frontline enablement programs. Recruitment cost savings of $5K–$50K per retained FTE depending on role complexity and market. Productivity gain from lower average tenure length of ~15%.
**Key assumptions:** Frontline attrition is experience/tools-driven (not compensation, culture, or management quality issues at a leadership level). Attrition rate is currently above industry benchmarks. Cost of turnover has been calculated or can be estimated.
**Evidence to seek:** Current attrition rate vs. industry benchmark, exit interview themes, eNPS data, cost-to-hire and time-to-productivity for frontline roles.
