# CX Initiative Prioritization: Intake Response
## EVAL-19 | prioritize | Prompt 2

---

To give you a defensible prioritization rather than a ranked list that falls apart under scrutiny, I need five inputs:

---

**1. The initiative list — what are you trying to prioritize?**

Write them out. One per line. A phrase or sentence each is fine — I'll ask one or two clarifying questions about any that are too vague to score.

Don't pre-filter: include everything you're considering, even the ones you suspect won't make the cut. The value of a portfolio view comes from seeing the full picture and making explicit choices, not from sanitizing the list before you start.

**2. Strategic priorities — what is the organization trying to achieve? (2–3 only)**

Not a list of 10 values. The 2–3 things that leadership would say if asked: "What are we actually trying to accomplish this year?" Common examples:
- Grow the premium customer segment
- Reduce cost-to-serve by 20%
- Improve NPS from -5 to +15
- Retain at-risk customers in [specific product category]

These become the scoring axis for Strategic Alignment. If you can't name 2–3 clear priorities, that's worth surfacing — it means the org doesn't have shared direction, and prioritization without shared direction is politics, not strategy.

**3. Constraints — what are the real limits?**

- Budget: Total available for the portfolio (not per initiative)
- Team: How many people, and what capability? (CX designers? Product managers? Engineers? All three?)
- Time horizon: When do you need to show results? Is there a board date, a review cycle, a public commitment?

Be honest about constraints. An artificially generous constraint produces an artificially ambitious portfolio that will fail in delivery.

**4. Non-negotiables — what can't be changed?**

Things that are already decided, politically protected, or outside the scope of this prioritization:
- "The CRM replacement is already funded and can't be stopped"
- "We've committed to the regulator that we'll complete X by [date]"
- "The CEO personally sponsored initiative Y — it's happening regardless"

I'll score non-negotiables for completeness but flag them separately — they're constraints to sequence around, not candidates for deprioritization.

**5. Success definition — how will you know the portfolio worked?**

Not "improved NPS." Specific: "NPS improves 10 points by end of year" or "cost-to-serve reduces by $2M" or "complaint volume drops 25%." This determines what "high impact" means when I score — without it, I'm guessing at what matters.

---

Once I have these five inputs, I'll flag any initiatives that need a clarifying question before I score, show you the full scoring table before drawing conclusions, and then give you a sequencing recommendation with explicit rationale for what you're not doing and why.

The most common failure in portfolio prioritization is trying to do everything. My job is to help you concentrate resources at the highest-leverage point — which means some good ideas won't make the cut, and I'll be direct about which ones and why.
