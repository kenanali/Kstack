# Grading Notes — Trend Scan Skill, Eval-0

Evaluating the skill definition against the two test prompts. These notes assess the skill's design quality, not just the output quality, because the outputs were generated by me performing the skill — the question is whether the SKILL.md would reliably elicit the same output from any competent LLM run.

---

## 1. What the Skill Did Well

**Specificity mandate is the strongest feature.** The instruction to refuse generic implications, with the concrete example contrasting bad ("customers will expect seamless digital experiences") against good ("AI-powered self-service tools in adjacent industries are training customers to expect real-time resolution without human contact — this company's 48-hour email response model will increasingly feel broken at the specific moment of billing disputes"), is the single most valuable line in the skill. It sets a behavioral standard that is hard to ignore and easy to self-evaluate against. Both outputs produced specific, mechanistic implications because of this mandate.

**The Step 2.5 check-in is well-designed.** The mandatory pause before implications is smart — it prevents the skill from burning through the full output before the user has a chance to correct the signal selection. The exact wording is provided, which reduces LLM interpretation variance.

**PESTLE + Tech Waves structure is well-calibrated for CX strategy.** Adding Tech Waves as a separate dimension (not subsumed under Technology) is correct — it forces the model to think about adoption timelines explicitly, not just list technology trends. Both outputs produced richer technology analysis as a result.

**Convergence Zones are the highest-value section.** The instruction to find intersection points where forces compound each other is where the most original analysis lives. Both outputs produced convergence zones that were non-obvious and actionable. This section differentiates the output from a standard trend list.

**Section 4 synthesizing paragraph works well.** Forcing a single paragraph that connects the trend picture back to the specific CX challenge named in Step 0 is good output discipline. Both responses produced a genuinely synthesizing final section rather than a summary of what was already said.

**The skill chains well.** The closing prompt to feed outputs into /journey-map, /synthesize, or /persona-build is a nice touch that reinforces the workflow architecture.

---

## 2. What the Skill Missed or Could Improve

**Step 2.5 creates an awkward interactive/non-interactive gap.** The skill mandates a stop-and-wait at Step 2.5, but in document/async contexts (like these eval runs), there is no clear instruction for how to proceed when the user has already provided all context upfront and there is no interactivity. The skill should include a fallback: "If the user has provided complete context upfront and there is no opportunity to wait for a response, note the check-in, state the assumption you are making, and proceed." Without this, different LLM runs will handle the gate differently — some will hard-stop (breaking the eval), some will silently skip it, some will note it and proceed.

**No guidance on output length calibration.** The skill produces very long outputs by default because the format is comprehensive. There is no instruction about when to compress (e.g., if fewer than 5 signals are highly relevant) or how to handle a narrow CX challenge (e.g., a single touchpoint redesign vs. a full transformation strategy). This will cause LLMs to produce equally verbose outputs for simple prompts as for complex ones.

**The scoring table instructions are underspecified.** Step 2 says score relevance as High/Med/Low and CX Disruption as 1–5, but does not explain how to choose. There is no anchor definition for what a "5" looks like vs. a "4" — meaning the scores will be inconsistent across runs and models. This makes the table feel authoritative but be somewhat arbitrary. The skill should provide 1–2 anchor examples: "5 = this force is actively changing what customers can get elsewhere, creating an expectation gap that is closing within 12 months; 1 = this force is real but has no direct mechanism to change customer behaviour or expectations."

**Step 0 questions are good but don't ask about competitive context.** Knowing who the closest competitors are (and what CX moves they are making) is essential to calibrating "relevance to this company." Both test prompts lacked competitor context, and the outputs compensated by inferring it (Athena/Tic:Toc for the bank; Rippling/Lattice for the B2B HR company), but the skill should explicitly ask: "Who are your 2–3 closest competitors, and are there any specific moves they've made recently that concern you?"

**No instruction for handling pre-existing user knowledge.** Prompt 2 says "I'm thinking mainly about AI, but I want to know if I'm missing anything." The skill has no explicit instruction for how to weight a user's existing focus vs. signals they haven't named. The output handled this well (flagging that AI is right but integration and compliance may be more immediately impactful), but this was judgment applied without skill guidance. The skill should include: "If the user has named a focus area, take it seriously but scan independently — flag if other dimensions score higher, and explain why."

**Signals on Watch section has weak trigger criteria in the skill definition.** The format calls for "the specific trigger that would elevate them" but provides no guidance on what a good trigger looks like. Both eval outputs wrote triggers, but they varied in quality (some were concrete, some were vague). A brief example in the skill definition would improve consistency.

**Section 4 lacks a word/sentence count anchor.** "4–6 sentences" is the instruction, which is fine, but the skill would benefit from a tone instruction: this paragraph should be written for a senior leader who will use it to brief a steering committee, not for the analyst who did the research. The current outputs are good but lean analytical; a senior-leader framing note would sharpen the synthesis toward decision-making language.

## 3. Specific Weaknesses to Fix in SKILL.md

**Fix 1: Add non-interactive fallback to Step 2.5**

After the check-in instruction, add:

> "If operating in a context where the user has provided complete upfront context and cannot respond interactively (e.g., document generation, batch processing, or the user has explicitly said 'go ahead'), note that the check-in is being skipped, state the top signals you are elevating, and proceed directly to Step 3."

**Fix 2: Add scoring anchors to Step 2**

Add a brief anchor table after the scoring criteria:

> CX Disruption Potential anchors:
> - 5 = This force is actively redefining what customers can receive elsewhere, creating a benchmark gap that will reach this company's customers within 12–18 months.
> - 3 = This force is changing background expectations over 2–4 years but is not yet creating acute comparison moments.
> - 1 = This force is real and relevant but will not directly change customer behaviour or expectations within the timeframe.

**Fix 3: Add competitor context to Step 0 intake questions**

Add to the Step 0 question list:
> "Who are your 2–3 closest competitors? Have any of them made notable CX moves recently that you're watching or concerned about?"

**Fix 4: Add instruction for handling user-named focus areas**

Add a sentence after the Step 0 section:

> "If the user has named a specific force they are already focused on (e.g., 'we're mainly thinking about AI'), include it fully in the scan but scan independently across all dimensions. If other signals score higher on relevance or disruption, name that tension explicitly — the most valuable output may be telling the user that their current focus is right but incomplete, or that a higher-urgency signal exists that they are not yet tracking."

**Fix 5: Add length calibration note**

After the Output Format section, add:

> "Length calibration: A full trend scan with 7–8 radar trends, 3 convergence zones, and 5 watch signals will typically run 2,000–3,500 words. For narrow CX challenges (single touchpoint, well-defined market), compress to 5–6 radar trends and 2 convergence zones. Avoid padding — if fewer than 5 signals are genuinely high-relevance, say so rather than filling the radar to 8."

**Fix 6: Add a senior-leader framing note to Section 4**

After the Section 4 instruction, add:

> "Write Section 4 for a senior leader (VP, C-suite) who will use it to brief a steering committee — prioritise decision-making language ('the most important investment implication is...', 'the single most dangerous risk is...') over analytical summary language."

**Fix 7: Add a trigger quality note to the Signals on Watch section**

After the Signals on Watch instruction, add:

> "Each trigger should be specific enough that someone could check it: a regulatory event, a competitor action, a data threshold, or a market milestone. 'When this becomes mainstream' is not a trigger. 'When [specific competitor] ships [specific capability] and receives strong analyst coverage' is a trigger."

---

## Overall Assessment

The skill is well-designed at the structural level — the PESTLE + Tech Waves framework, the specificity mandate, the convergence zones section, and the chain-to-next-skill closing are all strong. The main weaknesses are in operational edge cases (non-interactive contexts, pre-existing user focus, scoring anchors) and output quality calibration (length, senior-leader framing). These are fixable with targeted additions to the SKILL.md without restructuring the overall approach. The skill would benefit most from fixing issues 1 (non-interactive fallback) and 2 (scoring anchors), as these are the most likely to cause inconsistent outputs across LLM runs.
