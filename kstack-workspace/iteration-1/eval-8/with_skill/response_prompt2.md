## Why "Skipping the Persona" Produces a Bad Journey Map

A journey map is not a process diagram. Process diagrams document steps — what happens in what order. Journey maps document *experience* — what a specific type of person thinks, feels, and needs at each moment.

The two most important columns in the journey map are **Customer Thought** (internal monologue, specific and honest) and **Emotion Score** (actual emotional state, not assumed neutral). Both of these require knowing:

- **What kind of person is making this complaint?** A loyal customer of 12 years who feels let down experiences the complaint process entirely differently from an at-risk customer on the verge of leaving, or a first-time user who's confused about whether they have a legitimate complaint at all.
- **What is the complaint about?** A billing dispute, a service failure, a product fault, and a staff misconduct complaint are not the same emotional journey — even through the same process steps.
- **What does the customer need from the process beyond resolution?** Some customers need to be heard and acknowledged. Some need speed. Some need proof that it won't happen again. Without knowing this, the "opportunity" column becomes generic.

A journey map without a persona is a process diagram wearing a journey map's clothing. It will show you the steps but not tell you where the experience breaks or why.

## What I Need to Proceed

I can help you build a useful journey map with a minimal amount of input. Please tell me:

**1. Who is the most important customer type to map this for?**
Pick the one that is causing the most business pain right now — the one where complaints are either highest volume, most damaging to retention, or most escalation-prone. Examples:
- Long-term customers who are complaining for the first time and feel disappointed
- High-value customers whose complaints are driving churn
- Customers who complain repeatedly because first-contact resolution is failing
- Vulnerable customers (elderly, distressed, accessibility needs) where standard process creates additional harm

**2. What type of complaint are we focusing on?**
The emotional journey for a billing error complaint is structurally different from a product failure complaint. Narrowing this prevents the map from being too generic to design from.

**3. What do you already know is broken?**
Even without formal research — what are your frontline staff or complaints team saying? What are the most common reasons complaints escalate or reopen? This is evidence too.
