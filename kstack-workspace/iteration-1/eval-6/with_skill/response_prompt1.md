# Persona Build — Stressed First-Home Buyers: Mortgage Application

---

## Step 0: Context Loaded

**Segment:** First-home buyers going through a mortgage application at a bank
**Research available:**
- Survey: 73% describe process as "overwhelming"
- Qualitative complaints: "Too much paperwork," "Never knew where I was in the process," "Felt judged when I asked questions"
- Behavioral data: 67% start online then switch to branch; average 4.2 calls per application; 38% drop-off at income verification stage
- Trigger: recently received pre-approval from one bank, now shopping around

**Intended use:** Journey mapping, service design, stakeholder alignment

---

## Step 1: Jobs-to-be-Done

### Functional Jobs
- **Understand exactly which documents are required and when** — so I don't waste time gathering the wrong things or face unexpected requests mid-process [EVIDENCED: "Too much paperwork" complaint pattern; 38% drop-off at income verification stage suggests requirements are unclear or surprising]
- **Know where my application is at any given moment** — track status without having to call [EVIDENCED: "Never knew where I was in the process" complaint; 4.2 average calls per application signals status-checking behaviour driven by opacity]
- **Compare this lender's offer against alternatives** — confirm I'm getting a fair deal before committing [EVIDENCED: trigger event = shopping post-pre-approval]

### Emotional Jobs
- **Feel in control of a complex, high-stakes process** — not at the mercy of opaque systems [EVIDENCED: 73% "overwhelming"; "Never knew where I was" complaint]
- **Feel respected and not judged when asking questions** — especially for someone who has never done this before [EVIDENCED: "Felt judged when I asked questions" — verbatim complaint theme]
- **Feel like I made the smart, responsible choice** — the financial decision anxiety is as much about identity as money [INFERRED from context of first-home purchase as a milestone life event]

### Social Jobs
- **Be seen by family and peers as someone who handled this well** — first home purchase is a social milestone with audience pressure [INFERRED — not directly evidenced but consistent with life-stage research on first-home buyers]

---

## Step 2: Trigger Events

1. **Just received pre-approval from a bank and is now comparison shopping** — has proof of viability, now wants to confirm she's going with the right lender [EVIDENCED: stated in research]
2. **Income verification request arrives and creates confusion about what's needed** — the 38% abandonment at this stage suggests this is a distinct trigger for disengagement, not just a process step [EVIDENCED: behavioral drop-off data]
3. **Goes several days without status update and starts to worry the application has stalled** — the 4.2 average calls signals this is a recurring trigger throughout the process [INFERRED from call volume data]
4. **Friend or colleague mentions their own mortgage experience** — social comparison triggers anxiety about whether her process is normal [INFERRED — consistent with life-stage context]

---

## Step 3: Decision Factors and Anxieties

### Decision Factors (ranked)
1. **Clarity and transparency of the process** — not just rate; customers who feel overwhelmed will choose the lender who makes them feel most in control [INFERRED from dominance of process-related complaints over rate complaints in the research data]
2. **Interest rate / total cost** — functional comparison anchor; the trigger event is shopping around, so rate is clearly in play [INFERRED — rate not mentioned explicitly in research but is standard for this context]
3. **Speed and ease of approval** — reducing anxiety means preferring the path of least resistance [EVIDENCED: drop-off at income verification = friction drives abandonment]
4. **Whether staff treated them with respect** — "felt judged" complaint indicates branch interactions influence overall lender preference [EVIDENCED]

### Anxieties
- **Fear of being declined** — investing significant emotional and practical effort, then being told no, is a core vulnerability [INFERRED — not stated but consistent with high-stakes application context; evidenced indirectly by 73% "overwhelming" and checking behaviour]
- **Not knowing what I've missed or done wrong** — income verification drop-off at 38% is likely driven by fear of failing a step, not just complexity [INFERRED from behavioral data pattern]
- **Being judged for asking basic questions** — explicitly evidenced; customers are suppressing questions rather than seeking clarification [EVIDENCED: verbatim complaint]
- **Committing to the wrong lender** — choosing before comparing properly; triggers shopping behaviour [EVIDENCED: trigger event context]

---

## Step 4: Technology Comfort and Channel Preferences

**Technology comfort:** Moderate — comfortable enough to begin online (67% start there), but defaults to human contact when complexity or anxiety rises [EVIDENCED: 67% channel-switching pattern; 4.2 calls]

**Channel preferences by task type:**
- **Research and comparison (low stakes, own pace):** Website / online [EVIDENCED: 67% start online]
- **Complex document submission / application steps:** Branch — in-person contact preferred when stakes and confusion peak [EVIDENCED: channel-switch to branch pattern]
- **Status checking:** Phone (currently) — but this is driven by absence of digital alternatives, not preference [INFERRED from 4.2 call average; calls are compensating behaviour not a preference signal]
- **Proactive updates:** Would likely prefer SMS/email if available — currently forced into inbound calls [INFERRED]

**Digital frustrations:**
- Process status is not visible online — forces phone calls [EVIDENCED: call volume pattern]
- Document requirements not clearly communicated upfront — drives confusion and drop-off [EVIDENCED: income verification abandonment; "too much paperwork" complaints]

---

## Step 5: Unmet Needs

- **Sarah needs real-time visibility into where her application is and what comes next, but currently receives no proactive updates and must call the bank 4+ times to find out.** [EVIDENCED]
- **Sarah needs to know exactly which documents are required at each stage before she begins gathering them, but currently discovers requirements progressively and without warning — causing frustration and abandonment at income verification.** [EVIDENCED]
- **Sarah needs to feel safe asking questions about a process she's doing for the first time, but currently experiences bank staff interactions as subtly judgmental, which leads her to suppress questions and proceed with uncertainty.** [EVIDENCED]
- **Sarah needs to compare lenders on factors beyond rate — including process experience and service quality — but currently has no reliable way to assess this until she's already deep in an application.** [INFERRED]

## Designer's Note

The dominant design mistake this persona invites is fixing the paperwork burden without fixing the visibility problem — the research suggests that the *uncertainty* about status is as distressing as the volume of documents, if not more so. Sarah is not just overwhelmed by complexity; she is anxious because she receives no signal about whether she is doing it correctly, which is why she calls 4+ times. Second: the judgment anxiety is a suppressed behaviour signal — she is *not asking questions* when confused, which means drop-off and abandonment may be silent; she leaves rather than escalates. Designing more "helpful" prompts that feel condescending will backfire. Third: the channel switch to branch is anxiety-driven, not preference-driven — it is a failure signal disguised as engagement. A service improvement that keeps Sarah entirely online without reducing anxiety has not fixed the problem. Finally, rate is notably absent from the complaint data, which is worth flagging to stakeholders who may assume price is the primary driver.
