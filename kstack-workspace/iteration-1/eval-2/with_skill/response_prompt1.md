### Domain 1: Revenue & Growth

**Health Rating: AT RISK**

**Evidence Summary:**
Revenue has been flat for three years. In a telco context, flat revenue is not a neutral outcome — it typically represents erosion in real terms once inflation is applied, and it signals that the business is not growing its value base. The simultaneity of flat revenue and premium customer attrition suggests a structural shift is underway: the business may be retaining volume (headcount of customers) while degrading revenue quality (losing high-ARPU customers).

**Key Evidence:**
- Revenue flat for 3 consecutive years (user-stated; no financial documents provided)
- Premium customer segment losing customers to a new MVNO competitor

**Inferred (not evidenced):**
- If total revenue is flat but premium customers are leaving, either ARPU from remaining customers is holding via price increases, or low-value customer acquisition is offsetting premium losses — neither is a sustainable trajectory
- A flat revenue line in a market with inflation implies real revenue decline

**Data Gaps:**
- Revenue by segment (premium vs. base vs. prepaid) — without this, cannot determine whether the premium loss is being masked or offset
- ARPU trend — critical to understanding whether flat revenue reflects volume stability or a deteriorating customer mix
- Revenue breakdown by product/service line — determines where to focus CX intervention for revenue impact
- Whether any price increases have been implemented in the last 3 years (price-driven vs. volume-driven flat)

## Section 2: Key Tensions

**Tension 1: Revenue stability is masking customer quality deterioration.**
Total revenue has been flat for three years, which may appear manageable — but the simultaneous loss of premium customers suggests the revenue base is shifting downward in quality, with high-ARPU customers being replaced by lower-ARPU ones. The headline number is hiding a structural problem.

**Tension 2: NPS is declining fastest among the customers the business can least afford to lose.**
Premium customers — those with the highest lifetime value and lowest price sensitivity — are both most likely to notice experience degradation and most able to act on it by switching. A 14-point NPS drop in 18 months is not evenly distributed; it is almost certainly concentrated at the premium end, which is the same end now defecting to the MVNO.

**Tension 3: A $3M CX investment is being considered in a business that hasn't yet connected CX to operational cost.**
Without cost-to-serve data linked to experience quality, the business case risks being framed as "improving scores" rather than "reducing cost and protecting revenue" — a framing that is less compelling to a CFO and easier to deprioritize.

**Tension 4: The MVNO threat is accelerating at exactly the point when internal CX capability is least mature.**
New market entrants move fast; established telcos move slowly. If the CX transformation program takes 18-24 months to deliver outcomes, the MVNO will have had two more years to build its customer base from your premium segment — and brand reputation is very difficult to recover once the narrative shifts.

**Tension 5: The organization needs CX evidence to invest in CX capability, but lacks the measurement infrastructure to generate that evidence.**
The data gaps in this diagnostic — particularly in operational health and customer-level CLV — suggest that the measurement foundation required to prove CX ROI may not yet exist. The business case may need to include investment in measurement as a prerequisite to proving impact.

## Section 3: Strategic "So What"

The business is in the early stages of a premium customer exodus that flat revenue figures are currently masking. NPS has declined 14 points in 18 months — a pace that indicates a specific, addressable set of experience failures rather than ambient market dissatisfaction — and the MVNO taking your best customers is almost certainly winning on experience quality rather than price alone. The most urgent CX need is not broad transformation but rapid diagnosis: identifying the two or three touchpoints where premium customers are making their leave-or-stay decisions, and closing those gaps faster than the MVNO can consolidate its position.

Without CX intervention, the trajectory is clear: premium churn accelerates, ARPU declines, and the business becomes increasingly dependent on high-volume, low-value customers — a position that is both operationally expensive (higher contact rates, more failure demand) and strategically weak. The $3M investment is not a discretionary improvement program; it is a defence of the revenue base. Success looks like premium churn rate returning to pre-MVNO levels within 24 months, NPS recovering to the +20 range, and measurable reduction in cost-to-serve as experience quality reduces avoidable contact — three outcomes that together represent a return significantly exceeding the program investment.
