## Section 1: Five-Domain Diagnostic

---

### Domain 1: Revenue & Growth

**Health Rating: AT RISK**

**Evidence Summary:**
Revenue is up 12% YoY — a headline that looks healthy until you examine the mechanism. Growth is entirely price-driven, not volume-driven. Market share is flat, meaning no net new customers are being acquired. This is a brittle revenue position: the business is extracting more from an existing (and potentially shrinking in quality) customer base rather than growing it. Price-led revenue growth in a context of rising churn and falling NPS is a leading indicator of eventual revenue reversal — customers are tolerating higher prices now, but not improving their relationship with the business.

**Key Evidence:**
- Revenue +12% YoY (stated)
- Growth attributable to price increases, not volume (stated)
- Market share flat (stated)
- Churn rate rising: 5.1% → 8.2% over two years (stated)

**Inferred (not evidenced):**
- If churn is 8.2% and market share is flat, the business is acquiring new customers at roughly the same rate it's losing them — a high-cost, low-value growth pattern
- Price increases applied to a customer base with declining satisfaction and rising churn are a finite lever; at some point, price elasticity is reached and volume loss accelerates

**Data Gaps:**
- ARPU by customer segment — are price increases being applied evenly, or concentrated at a segment that is then churning?
- Revenue breakdown by customer cohort — are long-tenure, high-value customers churning at a higher rate than average?
- Forward-looking revenue risk — if churn continues at 8.2%+ and price increase capacity is limited, what does the revenue trajectory look like in 12-24 months?

### Domain 2: Customer Metrics

**Health Rating: CRITICAL**

**Evidence Summary:**
Every customer metric is deteriorating. NPS is down 15 points. Churn has risen 61% in relative terms (5.1% to 8.2%) over two years. These are not independent signals — they are the same underlying story told twice: customers are having a worse experience and leaving at higher rates. The pace of churn increase (3.1 percentage points in two years) is particularly alarming. At 8.2%, the business is replacing roughly 1 in 12 of its customers every year just to stay still — at significant acquisition cost.

**Key Evidence:**
- NPS: down 15 points (timeframe not specified)
- Churn rate: 8.2% (up from 5.1% two years ago — a +3.1pp / +61% relative increase)

**Inferred (not evidenced):**
- Churn at 8.2% is above-average for most B2C service industries; in sectors like telco or financial services, this would sit in the high-risk range
- NPS decline of 15 points typically takes 18-36 months to translate fully into churn acceleration — if NPS is still declining, churn is likely to continue rising even if nothing changes
- The business is currently in a deterioration cycle: poor experience → NPS decline → churn increase → pressure to acquire new customers at cost → less investment available for experience improvement

**Data Gaps:**
- NPS by segment and touchpoint — what is driving the decline?
- NPS trend over time — is the decline accelerating, steady, or beginning to flatten?
- Customer Lifetime Value trend — is the business retaining lower-value customers longer while higher-value customers leave?
- Complaint volume and resolution rate
- Customer Effort Score or CSAT — NPS alone doesn't diagnose root cause

### Domain 3: Operational Health

**Health Rating: CRITICAL**

**Evidence Summary:**
Cost-to-serve is up 22% with no measurable operational improvement. This is a double deterioration: costs are higher and outcomes are no better. In most service businesses, rising cost-to-serve without performance improvement indicates increasing failure demand — customers contacting more often because issues aren't being resolved, processes aren't working, or digital self-serve is failing. The 22% cost increase is almost certainly causally linked to the NPS decline and churn rise: the same underlying experience failures that dissatisfy customers also generate avoidable operational cost.

**Key Evidence:**
- Cost-to-serve: +22% (stated)
- No operational improvement metrics to justify the cost increase (stated)

**Inferred (not evidenced):**
- Cost-to-serve rising alongside NPS declining and churn rising is the classic failure demand pattern: broken experiences generate more contacts, escalations, and exceptions — all of which are expensive to handle
- A 22% cost increase with no productivity improvement suggests the cost is reactive (handling more demand) rather than proactive (investing in capability)
- If this pattern continues, the business faces a cost spiral: more churn → more acquisition needed → more onboarding contacts → more failure demand → higher cost-to-serve

**Data Gaps:**
- Breakdown of cost-to-serve increase — what is driving it? (Contact volume, headcount, third-party cost, technology?)
- Contact reason analysis — what proportion of contacts are avoidable / failure demand?
- First-contact resolution rate and repeat contact rate
- Digital self-serve adoption and containment rates
- Process error rates and rework volumes

**Evidence Summary:**
Market share is flat despite 12% revenue growth. This means price increases are generating revenue but not improving competitive position — the business is getting more money from the same relative slice of the market, not growing its footprint. Flat market share alongside rising churn implies the business is on a treadmill: churning customers at one end while acquiring new ones at the other, at net-zero market share but high gross acquisition and retention cost.

**Key Evidence:**

**Inferred (not evidenced):**

### Domain 5: Organizational Capability

**Health Rating: CRITICAL**

**Evidence Summary:**
Employee NPS of -12 is a significant red flag. A negative employee NPS means more employees are detractors than promoters — they would not recommend the organization as a place to work. In service businesses, employee experience and customer experience are tightly coupled: disengaged employees deliver inconsistent service, are less likely to resolve issues at first contact, and are more likely to leave (increasing turnover cost and eroding institutional knowledge). An Employee NPS of -12 does not just indicate a people problem — it indicates a systemic risk to the CX transformation itself.

**Key Evidence:**
- Employee NPS: -12 (stated)

**Inferred (not evidenced):**
- Negative Employee NPS in a service business is a leading indicator of deteriorating customer experience — it often precedes or amplifies customer NPS decline
- A CX transformation program attempting to improve customer experience while the workforce is actively disengaged faces a structural execution risk: changes designed at the top will not be consistently delivered at the frontline
- Employee NPS of -12 suggests leadership alignment, communication, and culture are all under strain

**Data Gaps:**
- Employee NPS by function — is the -12 concentrated in frontline / customer-facing roles (where it most directly affects CX) or back-office?
- Staff turnover rate and tenure data — high attrition compounds the capability problem
- Leadership alignment on CX as a priority
- Previous transformation history — has the organization attempted change programs before? What was the outcome?
- Capability and tooling — do frontline staff have the systems and authority to resolve customer issues effectively?

## Section 2: Key Tensions

**Tension 1: Revenue is growing through price increases applied to customers who are simultaneously becoming less satisfied and more likely to leave.**
The 12% revenue increase and the 15-point NPS decline and 61% churn increase are happening in the same business at the same time — the business is extracting more value per customer while delivering less. This is not a sustainable equilibrium; it is a delayed revenue cliff.

**Tension 2: Cost-to-serve is rising 22% while customer experience is deteriorating, which means the business is spending more to deliver less.**
This is not an underinvestment problem — money is being spent. It is a structural failure where operational spending is reactive and failure-driven rather than proactive and experience-improving. More money is going out, worse outcomes are coming back.

**Tension 3: The workforce is actively disengaged (Employee NPS -12) at the same moment the business needs to execute a customer experience transformation.**
A transformation that requires frontline behaviour change and sustained delivery improvement cannot succeed if the people responsible for delivery are detractors. The customer experience problem and the employee experience problem are the same problem, but they are likely being treated as separate ones.

**Tension 4: Market share is flat while churn is rising, meaning the business is running hard to stand still — acquiring customers to replace those it's losing, at a cost that compounds the operational pressure.**
The acquisition investment required to maintain flat market share while churn runs at 8.2% is significant and growing. Every customer acquired to replace a churned one is a cost that could have been avoided if experience had retained the original customer.

**Tension 5: The data available to diagnose the problem is insufficient to understand its root cause.**
NPS is down 15 points but the touchpoints driving the decline are unknown. Cost-to-serve is up 22% but the cost drivers are unknown. Churn is at 8.2% but the segments and reasons are unknown. The business has the scoreboard but not the game tape — and without root cause clarity, investment risks being directed at the wrong problems.

## Section 3: Strategic "So What"

This business is in a deterioration loop that the revenue line is currently masking. Price increases are generating 12% YoY revenue growth, but underneath that headline: customers are leaving at a 61% higher rate than two years ago, satisfaction has declined 15 points, operational costs are up 22% with no corresponding improvement, and the workforce is actively disengaged. The price lever is finite — once elasticity is reached, or once a competitor offers a credible alternative at a lower price point, the business will face simultaneous revenue and volume decline with no recovery mechanism in place.

CX intervention is urgent, but the problem is not primarily a "customer touchpoint" problem — it is an operational failure loop. The same broken processes that generate poor customer experiences are generating avoidable cost, which reduces the investment available to fix the experience, which worsens the experience further. The priority is not surface-level experience improvement; it is diagnosing and eliminating the root-cause failures that drive both dissatisfaction and unnecessary cost. Success in this context looks like: churn rate returning toward 5%, Employee NPS moving from negative into positive territory (a prerequisite, not a byproduct, of customer NPS recovery), and cost-to-serve growth decelerating as failure demand is reduced — three outcomes that together protect revenue, reduce cost, and rebuild the growth platform that price increases alone cannot sustain.
