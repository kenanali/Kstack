# Grading Notes: workshop-prep
## EVAL-14 and EVAL-15

---

## What the Skill Did Well

**1. Audience calibration is specific and design-informed.**
The skill correctly identified that executives with no HMW/journey-mapping experience require different scaffolding than designers, and built that into every activity choice. The decision to use silent brainstorm and Crazy 8s specifically because they neutralize dominant voices (the CFO) is the right facilitation logic — the skill connected the constraint to the method, not just acknowledged the constraint.

**2. The CFO constraint was operationalized, not just noted.**
The skill didn't just flag "the CFO will dominate" — it designed around it. Specifically: silent activities prevent verbal anchoring; dot voting happens before Impact/Effort 2×2 to preserve authentic preferences before financial framing enters; "effort" was pre-defined to include time and complexity, not just cost. This is practitioner-level thinking.

**3. The arc structure is sound and properly sequenced.**
Diverge before converge is maintained. The arc never jumps to prioritization without ideation. The Commitment Grid is correctly placed as the close, and the skill correctly identifies "we should look into this" as an unacceptable output.

**4. Facilitation notes are specific and risk-aware.**
The notes name specific failure modes (not generic "keep energy up" advice), name specific facilitation moves, and identify which participant is most likely to trigger each failure mode. The distinction between "what to say when the CFO anchors financially during divergence" vs. "during convergence" shows calibrated thinking.

**5. HMW questions are grounded and tied to named research tensions.**
The five HMW questions are specific, tied to named findings, and avoid the common trap of leading toward a solution. HMW 3 (early indication of approval likelihood) and HMW 5 (decline experience redesign) are particularly well-formed — they open up solution space rather than narrowing it.

**6. The pre-read brief is appropriate for the audience.**
Under 400 words, plain language, no jargon, ends with a genuine reflection prompt. The three research insights are substantive enough to prepare executives without overwhelming them. The explanation of "you'll be guided through every step" is the right framing for an executive who has never done HMW work.

**7. Prompt 2 (intake response) was correctly handled.**
The skill resisted the vague prompt and asked structured questions that are genuinely necessary for design, rather than either producing a generic template or asking unnecessary questions. The six intake questions are well-ordered and each is justified.

## What the Skill Missed or Did Poorly

**1. The Objective Confirmation step (Step 0.5) was not executed as a genuine gate.**
The skill definition requires explicitly restating the objective and key constraint, then asking for confirmation before proceeding. The response proceeded directly into full design. In a real interactive session this would be appropriate if an earlier message confirmed, but the skill should have either shown the confirmation step explicitly or noted that it was skipping it and why. Evaluators looking for the mandatory checkpoint will not find it.

**2. The Research Readout duration may be too long for this specific group.**
At 25 minutes with 12 people — including 4 executives who are likely the most time-constrained — the readout risks running over and compressing convergence time. The activity library flags: "If the session is 2 hours or less, compress to 15 minutes max." This is a 3-hour session, but with 4 executives who are unfamiliar with the methods and prone to tangents, 20 minutes with a hard cutoff would be safer. The skill should have flagged this tension explicitly.

**3. No explicit small-group mixing instruction for Crazy 8s.**
The skill flags the risk of seniority-based self-sorting and addresses it in Watch-Out 2 — but only for small-group activities generically. The specific instruction to mix executives and designers for the Crazy 8s table clusters is missing from the runsheet itself. A facilitator reading the runsheet without the Watch-Outs might miss this.

**4. The Break placement is late relative to energy management.**
A 10-minute break at 10:30 (90 minutes in) is at the outer edge of sustained cognitive engagement for a group that includes senior executives unfamiliar with creative methods. In practice, energy drops are detectable earlier. The skill should note a secondary energy-management strategy for the 50–60 minute mark (e.g., a movement prompt, a table rotation, or a change of pace activity) rather than relying on a single mid-session break.

**5. The Idea Share / Wall Build activity at 10:40 is underspecified.**
The instructions ("3–5 second verbal label for each sticky as they post") could produce chaos with 12 people and potentially 40–60+ stickies. For a group of this size and composition, the facilitator needs a clearer protocol: do people post simultaneously or in sequence? How does the facilitator manage the session when all 12 are at the wall at once? The Watch-Outs don't address this.

**6. No explicit note about what happens to the output after the session.**
The Commitment Grid captures next steps, but the skill doesn't address what format the full output takes (photographs? transcribed document? summary deck?) or who is responsible for producing it and in what timeframe. For an executive workshop, the output documentation protocol matters — executives often refer back to "what we agreed" and the lack of a clean record creates ambiguity. This is a gap in the output design, not just facilitation notes.

**7. Hybrid/remote contingency is not addressed.**
The prompt specifies in-person, so this is not a flaw — but the skill should proactively note it has assumed full in-person and flag what would change if the format shifted (e.g., if an executive needs to dial in). In practice, a "confirmed in-person" often becomes "one person on video" on the day.

## Specific Weaknesses to Fix in the Skill Definition

1. **Make Step 0.5 (Objective Confirmation) a visible, formatted output** — not just an instruction in the skill definition. The skill should produce the confirmation text explicitly (even in a simulated non-interactive evaluation run) so it's auditable. Consider: "**Before proceeding:** The workshop objective as I've understood it is [X]. The key constraint is [Y]. I'm proceeding on this basis — please correct if anything is wrong."

2. **Add a duration sensitivity check** — when the audience includes very senior executives unfamiliar with CX methods, flag explicitly if any single activity exceeds 20 minutes without a built-in interaction point. The skill has the principle but doesn't apply it consistently.

3. **Add a post-workshop output protocol** to the Output Format. Something like: "Section 8: Post-Session Documentation Plan — who captures what, in what format, by when." This is a practical gap that facilitators hit every time.

4. **Add a "what changes if one executive dials in" Watch-Out** as a standard item when the format is in-person. This is almost always relevant in practice.
