# Workshop Design: Mortgage Application Journey Ideation
## EVAL-14 | workshop-prep | Prompt 1

---

## Objective Confirmation

**Workshop objective:** By the end of this 3-hour session, the group of 12 — including 4 senior executives and 8 service designers — will have generated a shortlisted set of ideas (10–15) for reducing mortgage application journey friction, evaluated them against a shared impact/effort lens, and committed to a prioritized shortlist of 3–5 ideas to progress to detailed development.

**Key constraint:** The CFO will dominate financial ROI framing during convergence activities. The design must structure divergence so that no single voice (especially financial) narrows the idea space before generation is complete.

---

## Section 1: Success Criteria

1. By the end, participants will have **generated at least 40 distinct ideas** across 3–4 HMW questions, with meaningful contributions from both executives and designers (not dominated by one group).
2. By the end, the group will **share alignment on a prioritized shortlist of 3–5 friction-reduction ideas**, each with a named executive sponsor and a design team owner.
3. The output of this workshop will be **a shortlisted idea set with preliminary impact/effort scoring**, usable as direct input to a `/biz-case` or `/prioritize` session within the following week.
4. By the end, every participant — including executives who have never done HMW or journey work — will **understand the top 3 friction tensions** from the synthesis research and will be able to articulate why they matter to customers and to the business.

---

## Section 2: Workshop Arc

```
OPEN                    FRAME                   DIVERGE
─────────────           ───────────────         ──────────────────────
Check-In Round    →    Research Readout   →    HMW Silent Brainstorm
(Human moment,         (5 key tensions         (Per-question, silent,
not logistics)          made tangible           individual — no
                        for executives)         groupthink)
                              │
                              ▼
                        HMW Question
                        Review + Selection
                        (group picks focus)

DIVERGE (cont.)         CONVERGE               CLOSE
──────────────          ───────────────        ─────────────────────
Crazy 8s           →   Dot Voting         →   Commitment Grid
(structured             (silent, before         (named owners,
sketching to            financial framing       specific actions,
break safe ideas)       anchors group)          dates)
                              │
                              ▼
                        Impact vs. Effort 2×2
                        (collaborative sort —
                        disagreement surfaced
                        and used)
                              │
                              ▼
                        One-Word Close
```

**Arc design rationale:**
- Executives without HMW familiarity are introduced to the method *after* seeing the research — the research creates the motivation to use the method rather than requiring them to trust an abstract framework first.
- Silent brainstorm and Crazy 8s are specifically designed to prevent the CFO from anchoring the group financially before ideas are generated. The CFO cannot dominate a silent activity.
- Dot voting happens before the Impact vs. Effort 2×2 to capture authentic preferences before financial framing enters the room.
- The Commitment Grid forces specificity — "we should look into this" is not acceptable output.

## Section 4: HMW Questions

*Grounded in synthesis outputs from the mortgage application journey. Each question is tied to a specific identified tension.*

**HMW 1:** How might we keep mortgage applicants informed about their application status in a way that reduces their anxiety — without requiring them to chase us?
*Responds to tension: Applicants describe a "black hole" experience after submission — they have no idea what is happening, and uncertainty drives repeated inbound contact and abandonment.*

**HMW 2:** How might we make the document upload and verification process feel as simple as sending an email — even for first-time homebuyers?
*Responds to tension: Document requirements are communicated inconsistently, with customers frequently discovering additional requirements mid-process, creating distrust and delay.*

**HMW 3:** How might we give applicants a clear, honest sense of their likelihood of approval before they invest significant time in the application — without creating a compliance risk?
*Responds to tension: Customers who are declined late in the process experience significant emotional distress and reputational damage accrues; early indication could reduce wasted effort on both sides.*

**HMW 4:** How might we empower frontline staff to resolve application queries on the first call — rather than having to escalate or call back?
*Responds to tension: Staff report being unable to access real-time application status information, leading to repeat contacts and agent frustration. This is a structural empowerment issue, not a training one.*

**HMW 5:** How might we redesign the moment when an application is declined so that customers leave with a path forward — rather than feeling dismissed?
*Responds to tension: Declined applicants are a significant source of negative word-of-mouth and low NPS. The decline experience is currently scripted and impersonal.*

## Section 5: Materials Checklist

**Room setup:**
- [ ] Tables arranged in clusters of 3–4 (not theatre-style or boardroom-style) — enables small-group work
- [ ] Wall space cleared and accessible for posting (minimum 3 large wall areas)
- [ ] A4 paper, one sheet per person (for Crazy 8s)
- [ ] Markers — not ballpoints, not pencils. Thick markers. One per person minimum, spares available.
- [ ] Large sticky notes (3 colours, ~30 per person = ~360 total)
- [ ] Small dot stickers, 5 per person (60 total, plus spares)
- [ ] Large 2×2 matrix drawn/printed on paper or whiteboard
- [ ] Commitment Grid template on whiteboard or large paper
- [ ] Timer (visible to room — projected or physical)

**Printed materials:**
- [ ] 1-page tension summary (one per participant — the 5 key synthesis tensions)
- [ ] HMW questions printed A3 and posted on wall before session starts
- [ ] Pre-read brief distributed 3–5 days before

**Digital:**
- [ ] Slides for Research Readout (5 tensions with customer quotes + data)
- [ ] Photo capture plan — assign someone to photograph wall outputs at every stage

**Food + logistics:**
- [ ] Coffee/water available from start (not just at break)
- [ ] Break refreshments ready
- [ ] No phones-on-table norm set at opening — facilitator sets the tone by placing own phone away

## Section 6: Pre-Read Brief

**TO:** [Participants]
**FROM:** [Facilitator name]
**SUBJECT:** Mortgage Journey Workshop — What to Expect and How to Prepare
**DATE:** [3–5 days before session]

---

**Why we're meeting**

We're spending 3 hours together to generate and prioritize ideas for improving how customers experience our mortgage application process. This isn't a review meeting or a strategy presentation. It's a working session — you'll be creating things, not just watching things.

**What the research tells us**

Over the past [X weeks], we've mapped the mortgage application journey with customers and internal teams. Three things stand out:

1. **Customers feel abandoned after they apply.** Once an application is submitted, they receive little to no proactive communication — many describe it as a "black hole." This drives repeat inbound calls and, in some cases, abandonment.

2. **Document requirements surprise people mid-process.** Customers frequently discover additional requirements after they've started — creating distrust, delay, and significant drop-off at the verification stage.

3. **Staff can't answer questions in real time.** Frontline teams don't have visibility into live application status — so customers who do call in often get told "I'll call you back," which rarely happens on time.

**What the day looks like**

We'll move through three phases: understanding the problem together, generating ideas, and agreeing on what to pursue. The methods we use (including one that involves sticky notes and another that involves sketching quickly) are designed to generate better ideas than a standard meeting format. If you haven't done this before, don't worry — you'll be guided through every step.

**What we need from you**

Come ready to think, not just observe. All roles — from CEO to designer — contribute equally in this session.

**Before you arrive, reflect on this question:**

*Think of a process — anywhere, not necessarily in our industry — where you felt genuinely well-guided as a customer. What made it feel that way? What would it take to bring that quality to our mortgage application experience?*

See you on [date]. Any questions, contact [facilitator].

## Section 7: Facilitator Watch-Outs

**Watch-Out 1: The CFO anchors financial evaluation before ideation is complete**
*Risk:* During the Research Readout or HMW Review, the CFO frames every problem as a cost/ROI question, narrowing what the group feels "safe" to ideate on.
*Mitigation:* Explicitly defer financial evaluation: "Cost and ROI are exactly what the second half of the session is built to address. Right now we're in creative mode — the only criterion is whether it would help the customer." If it continues, use a physical "parking lot" to acknowledge the point and visibly defer it: "Let's put that on the parking lot — we'll bring it to the 2×2 where it belongs."

**Watch-Out 2: Executives and designers self-sort and never interact**
*Risk:* The 8 designers quietly produce most of the ideas; the 4 executives observe or dominate rather than contribute. The session produces a designer-owned output that executives don't feel invested in.
*Mitigation:* Mix groups deliberately. At every small-group activity, assign groups with at least one executive and two designers per table. Name this explicitly: "I've mixed roles intentionally — the combination of operational perspective and customer perspective is where the best ideas live."

**Watch-Out 3: The Research Readout becomes a presentation — not shared context**
*Risk:* The facilitator or a team member presents for 25 minutes without check-ins. Executives lose engagement or start challenging methodology rather than absorbing findings.
*Mitigation:* Break up the readout with three mandatory interaction points: after tension 2 ("What surprises you?"), after tension 4 ("What confirms what you already knew?"), after all 5 ("What's the most uncomfortable one?"). Prepare to redirect data challenges: "That's a fair question about the data — I want to park it for now and follow up directly. The pattern across customers is consistent and that's what we're responding to today."

**Watch-Out 4: Crazy 8s produces resistance from senior participants**
*Risk:* The CEO, CFO, or COO visibly disengages during Crazy 8s, undermining psychological safety for the rest of the group.
*Mitigation:* Before the activity, frame the resistance directly: "This will feel uncomfortable for about 90 seconds, and then it won't. The discomfort is the point — it means your brain is past the obvious ideas." Sit near the most likely resister and model enthusiastic participation yourself. Have a fallback: if someone truly won't engage, let them write words instead of sketches — the constraint (8 ideas, 8 minutes) is what matters.

**Watch-Out 5: Commitment Grid produces vague commitments**
*Risk:* The session ends with excitement but no specific actions. "Let's set up a working group to look at this" is accepted without a name, a date, or a mandate.
*Mitigation:* Before the activity, say: "The test for a good commitment is: if I call you in three weeks and ask what happened with this, you can tell me exactly what was done, by whom, and what the result was. Anything that doesn't pass that test isn't a commitment yet." For each action, ask specifically: "Who owns this — one name? When will they have something to share? What do they need from others in this room to make that happen?" Do not move on until you have a name and a date.
