# Blocker Taxonomy

A reference taxonomy of CX blockers for use with the `/scan-blockers` skill. Organized into 8 categories across internal and external dimensions.

---

## INTERNAL BLOCKERS

### 1. Culture & Leadership

- Risk aversion / fear of failure — unwillingness to test, pilot, or iterate without certainty of outcome
- Low customer empathy in leadership — decisions made based on internal logic rather than customer evidence
- Siloed "my department" thinking — protecting departmental metrics over end-to-end customer outcomes
- Short-termism — quarterly targets and financial pressure overriding long-term CX investment
- Change fatigue / transformation burnout — org has been through too many programs; cynicism about new initiatives
- Low psychological safety — staff don't surface problems up the chain; issues stay hidden until they're critical
- CX seen as "soft" or marketing — experience improvement not treated as a business performance lever
- Senior sponsor fragility — CX program depends on a single executive champion; vulnerable to leadership change
- Vision cascade failure — the CX strategic direction is clear at leadership level but has not translated into how working teams prioritize, decide, or measure their daily work; frontline teams cannot connect their activities to the org's stated CX vision
- Execution-ideation imbalance — the org generates strong strategy and ideas but lacks the mechanisms, mandate, or appetite to prototype and test quickly; good thinking accumulates as documentation and workshops rather than live capability

### 2. Organizational Structure

- Fragmented journey ownership — no single accountable owner of the end-to-end customer experience across the key journey
- No CX function or weak CX mandate — no team or role with authority to set standards, govern quality, and drive improvement
- P&L structures creating internal competition — business units optimizing their own metrics at the expense of the whole customer
- Matrix org creating decision paralysis — too many sign-offs, no clear decision rights, everything goes to committee
- Frontline staff disempowered — rules and systems force staff to say "I can't help you with that"
- No cross-functional governance — no forum where product, ops, service, digital, and marketing align on customer experience
- Distance between strategy and frontline — leadership has no direct signal from frontline experience; decisions made in vacuum
- Decision rights ambiguity — no clear map of who can approve, prioritize, or reject CX work at each level; decisions default to escalation or committee not because they're strategic, but because no one has been explicitly given the authority to decide
- Competing team KPIs — teams required to collaborate on CX delivery have success metrics that pull in different directions; each team can be individually performing while the collective customer outcome is poor; the failure belongs to no one and the incentive to fix it belongs to no one

### 3. Process & Operations

- Processes designed for internal efficiency, not customer outcomes — optimized for throughput or cost, creating friction for customers
- High handoff count — too many transfers between teams, channels, or systems creating loss of context and error
- No feedback loop from complaints to improvement — issues raised, closed, not actioned; pattern never surfaced
- Manual workarounds masking systemic failures — staff heroics are hiding broken processes from leadership view
- Inconsistent delivery across channels or regions — customer experience varies significantly based on which channel or location they use
- Slow or absent escalation paths — complex or edge-case situations have no clear resolution path; customers trapped
- SLA focus over outcome focus — meeting the process metric (e.g., call answered in X seconds) but not the customer outcome

### 4. Technology & Data

- Legacy systems with no integration capability — core systems can't talk to each other; data doesn't flow; workarounds required
- Fragmented customer data — no single customer view; customer identity, history, and preferences spread across multiple systems
- No real-time CX signal — experience issues only visible through lagging surveys or monthly reports, not live operational data
- Analytics capability gap — data is collected but not actioned; reports produced but insights not used to change decisions
- Low digital channel capability — digital experience is behind customer expectations; forcing customers to lower-preference channels
- Vendor roadmap dependency — critical CX capabilities blocked by third-party vendor timelines outside org control
- Data quality issues — incorrect, incomplete, or stale data undermining personalization, service, and operational decisions

### 5. People & Capability

- Low CX / service design capability — no internal expertise in journey mapping, service design, or experience research
- High frontline turnover — institutional knowledge lost constantly; staff not in role long enough to be effective
- Training not connected to CX outcomes — compliance and product training, but not how to deliver great experience
- Incentives misaligned with CX — KPIs reward efficiency (call time, cases closed) not customer outcomes (resolution, effort)
- No career path for CX practitioners — can't attract or retain experience design talent
- Change management capability gap — ability to design and land change with frontline teams is underdeveloped

---

## EXTERNAL BLOCKERS

### 6. Regulatory & Compliance

- Data privacy regulations — GDPR, CCPA, and sector-specific rules limit personalization, data sharing, and retention
- Industry-specific regulations — financial services, health, utilities, and other regulated sectors face constraints on service design options
- Procurement and legal approval cycles — contract, legal review, and procurement processes slow down vendor selection and CX delivery
- Compliance requirements creating mandatory friction — regulated disclosures, consent flows, or verification steps that create poor experience but cannot be removed
- Accessibility requirements — legal obligations that require investment in accessible design (also an opportunity if addressed proactively)

### 7. Market & Competitive

- Commoditization pressure — competitive dynamics reduce investment case for CX differentiation ("customers just want cheapest")
- Cross-industry expectation setting — Amazon, Apple, and other leaders have set customer expectations that exceed what the org can deliver
- Partner / distributor channel dependency — significant customer touchpoints controlled by third parties outside direct influence
- Third-party data signal loss — loss of tracking data, cookies, or platform access reducing ability to understand and reach customers
- Competitive CX investment — competitors are visibly investing in CX, raising the table stakes

### 8. Economic & Environmental

- Budget constraints / cost reduction — CX investment competing directly with cost reduction mandates
- Macroeconomic pressures on customers — economic conditions changing customer priorities, reducing engagement, or shifting channel preferences
- Supply chain / operational constraints — service delivery limitations driven by supply-side issues (staff availability, infrastructure, materials)
- Post-disruption behaviour shifts — lasting changes in customer behaviour (e.g., post-pandemic channel preferences) that the org hasn't adapted to
- Sustainability and ESG demands — growing customer and regulatory expectations around environmental and social responsibility creating new experience requirements
